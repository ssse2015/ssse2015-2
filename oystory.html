Oyster reefs covered a vast area of New York Harbor and served as the main filters of the New York Harbor. Some biologists estimate that the New York Harbor contained half of the world's oysters. One oyster could filter up to 50 gallons of water, allowing the water to be a clear as possible. Oysters natural enemies include the blue crab and snails, but their real killer is polluted water and toxic water. Harbor oyster shells measured up to 10 inches, and early European travellers describe the shellfish as being about a foot in length. To grow to that length, those oysters would have been 10+ years old.

People discovered that oysters were edible, and delicious to some. Many seafood restaurants opened up and the oyster population dwindled. Increasing pollution and dump of waste to the Harbor also added to the problems the oysters faced. Burdened by over-harvesting, sewage pollution, and landfill, the last of the New York oyster beds was closed in 1927. The Clean Water Act of 1972, made it illegal for people to dump trash into the water without a permit. (Learn more.) Despite all the positive actions being taken to protect their habitat, the water had been so polluted that the oysters were rendered inedible. In addition to this, their population continued dropping since the water was still filthy. The Billion Oyster Project was formed in 2010 by Murray Fisher to allow oysters to return to their natural habitats and gradually restore the water to its original state.
